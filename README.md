        <h1>Part I: Executive Summary &amp; Investment Thesis</h1>
<h2>Executive Summary</h2>
<p>The Intergenerational Mobility Fund is a proposed philanthropy-anchored social impact vehicle designed to connect underemployed youth with older adults seeking purposeful cross-generational relationships, while creating pathways into jobs that pay meaningfully above minimum wage. The fund would back nonprofits, social enterprises, and community partnerships that combine paid mentorship, workforce training, and relationship-centered programming in a single model.</p>
<p>The investment thesis is straightforward: youth need social capital, coaching, and labor-market access; older adults need connection, purpose, and structured ways to contribute; and communities need scalable workforce models that improve earnings and reduce isolation at the same time. A blended capital structure can support pilot design, early operating losses, and growth financing while positioning successful programs to attract public workforce dollars, employer contracts, and outcomes-based capital over time.</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>Intergenerational programs already demonstrate that structured relationships between youth and older adults can strengthen belonging, mentorship, and community participation. At the same time, public and philanthropic funding streams exist for youth mentoring, workforce development, and community-based innovation, but they are typically fragmented and rarely organized around a unified employment-and-relationship model.</p>
<p>This fragmentation creates a market gap. Many youth employment initiatives focus narrowly on technical training, while many senior engagement programs focus on social connection without an earned-income pathway for younger participants. The proposed fund addresses both problems by financing models where intergenerational trust becomes an input to higher wages, retention, and long-term upward mobility.</p>
<h2>Fund Thesis</h2>
<p>The fund would invest in organizations that produce two outcomes at once: stronger employment outcomes for youth and stronger social connection for older adults. Rather than treating mentorship as a side benefit, the model treats sustained cross-generational relationships as core infrastructure for labor-market advancement.</p>
<p>Priority target populations would include youth ages 16 to 30 who are underemployed, working in low-wage jobs, or disconnected from career pathways, alongside adults age 60 and older who want meaningful, recurring roles as mentors, coaches, trainers, or community guides. Programs could focus on sectors with accessible wage progression such as healthcare support, community health, skilled trades, digital support, green jobs, and neighborhood-based service roles.</p>
<h2>Proposed Fund Structure</h2>
<p>A credible pilot vehicle would launch at $5–15 million as a blended-finance fund with philanthropic first-loss capital and flexible recoverable grants, paired with senior capital from mission-aligned institutions. This structure fits the early-stage risk profile of community-centered workforce models that need time to prove both revenue durability and impact performance.</p>

<h3>Investment Instruments</h3>
<p>The fund could use a mix of seed grants, recoverable grants, low-interest working capital loans, and revenue-share agreements depending on the maturity of each organization. Early-stage pilots would likely need grant-like capital, while scaled operators with contracts from employers, schools, or public agencies could absorb repayable financing.</p>
<h2>What the Fund Would Back</h2>
<h3>1. Intergenerational Workforce Academies</h3>
<p>These programs would pair sector-based training with recurring older-adult mentorship, helping youth build both technical competency and workplace judgment. Revenue could come from state workforce grants, employer-sponsored cohorts, placement fees, and public mentoring dollars.</p>
<h3>2. Community Service Enterprises</h3>
<p>These models would organize youth and elders into teams that deliver useful services such as digital assistance, health navigation, storytelling projects, neighborhood outreach, or light community-based support. They are attractive because they create earned revenue while reinforcing the intergenerational relationship at the center of the model.</p>
<h3>3. Intergenerational Hubs and Platforms</h3>
<p>These organizations would operate physical or hybrid spaces where youth and older adults meet regularly for job clubs, mentorship circles, project-based learning, and referral into work opportunities. Institutional customers could include libraries, schools, senior centers, hospitals, municipalities, and local employers seeking stronger community workforce pipelines.</p>
<h2>Revenue Model</h2>
<p>The strongest feature of the concept is that it does not rely on a single payer. Instead, it braids multiple funding streams that already exist but are rarely integrated into one operating model.</p>
<p>Key revenue sources include:
- Public mentoring grants that support structured youth-adult relationship programs
- State and local workforce grants that fund training, placement, and retention efforts
- Employer contracts for talent pipeline development, pre-apprenticeship, and retention support
- Philanthropic grants for intergenerational innovation and age-friendly community programming
- Service revenue from community-based projects delivered by youth-elder teams</p>
<p>Over time, the fund could help grantees or portfolio organizations transition from grant dependence toward mixed revenue bases with higher predictability and stronger repayment capacity.</p>

<p>A social impact fund pitch is strongest when outcomes are concrete and auditable. The fund should track youth economic mobility and older-adult well-being as parallel headline metrics.</p>

<h2>Example Pilot</h2>
<p>A strong first pilot would be an Intergenerational Healthcare Pathways Academy in a mid-sized city with a large hospital system, community college partners, and senior volunteer or retiree networks. Youth participants would train for roles such as medical assistant, patient access specialist, community health worker, or care navigator, while retired nurses, administrators, and social workers serve as paid mentors and workshop leaders.</p>
<p>The fund could provide a recoverable grant for launch, a working capital line for cohort delivery, and technical assistance on employer contracting and outcomes reporting. In this model, the social return is not only stronger wages for youth and reduced isolation for elders, but also a more community-rooted workforce pipeline for local healthcare institutions.</p>
<h2>Why a Social Impact Fund Should Back This</h2>
<p>This concept sits at the intersection of three proven funding priorities: youth opportunity, healthy aging, and community resilience. It offers a differentiated way to translate intergenerational connection into measurable economic outcomes, which is often missing from traditional mentoring or aging programs.</p>
<p>For a social impact fund, the opportunity is especially compelling because catalytic capital can unlock later-stage revenue from public systems and employers. That makes the model suitable for an incubator approach: philanthropic dollars absorb design risk early, while successful operators graduate toward more durable contracted revenue and repayable capital over time.</p>
<h2>Use of Proceeds</h2>
<p>An initial fund could allocate capital across four uses:
- <strong>35%</strong> for seed grants and design-stage pilots to test partnership models, matching protocols, and sector focus
- <strong>25%</strong> for working capital and recoverable grants to support first cohorts and employer contracting
- <strong>20%</strong> for technical assistance, including measurement systems, compliance support, and fundraising readiness
- <strong>20%</strong> as follow-on catalytic capital for the strongest portfolio organizations</p>
<h2>Closing Case</h2>
<p>The Intergenerational Mobility Fund is designed to finance a simple but underbuilt idea: young people move further and faster when they have real relationships, practical guidance, and access to better jobs, while older adults thrive when they have meaningful roles in the economic and social life of their communities. By backing organizations that intentionally convert cross-generational relationships into wage growth, job retention, and community value, a social impact fund can create a portfolio with both human and financial durability.</p>
